POLICE ACCOUNTABILITY AND CIVIL RIGHTS LAW
Federal Constitutional Rights and Local Accountability Mechanisms
Source: U.S. Constitution, 42 U.S.C. § 1983, Federal and State Case Law
Last Updated: 2024

================================================================================
FIRST AMENDMENT RIGHT TO RECORD POLICE — U.S. Const. Amend. I
================================================================================

The First Amendment to the United States Constitution protects the right to
record police officers performing their duties in public places.

LEGAL BASIS:
- Multiple federal Circuit Courts of Appeal have confirmed this right:
  * First Circuit: Glik v. Cunniffe, 655 F.3d 78 (1st Cir. 2011)
  * Third Circuit: Fields v. City of Philadelphia, 862 F.3d 353 (3d Cir. 2017)
  * Fifth Circuit: Turner v. Lieutenant Driver, 848 F.3d 678 (5th Cir. 2017)
  * Seventh Circuit: ACLU of Illinois v. Alvarez, 679 F.3d 583 (7th Cir. 2012)
  * Ninth Circuit: Fordyce v. City of Seattle, 55 F.3d 436 (9th Cir. 1995)
- The right extends to bystanders, journalists, and concerned citizens

YOUR RIGHTS WHEN RECORDING POLICE:
1. You may record officers in any PUBLIC place where you have a legal right
   to be (sidewalks, parks, public streets, government buildings' public areas)
2. You do not need the officer's permission to record
3. Officers MAY NOT lawfully demand you stop recording, delete footage,
   or surrender your phone without a valid warrant
4. Officers MAY ask you to stand back to avoid interfering — you must comply
   with reasonable distance requests (but not demands to stop recording)
5. You may record even if asked not to

WHAT OFFICERS CANNOT DO:
- Order you to stop recording (without arrest)
- Seize your phone without a warrant (Riley v. California, 573 U.S. 373 (2014))
- Require you to delete footage
- Arrest you solely for recording police (violates First Amendment)
- Obstruct your ability to record by physically blocking your view

IF YOUR PHONE IS SEIZED:
- Officers need a WARRANT to search the contents of your phone
  (Riley v. California, 573 U.S. 373 (2014) — unanimous Supreme Court ruling)
- Demand to know: "Am I under arrest? Are you seizing my phone?"
- Do NOT voluntarily consent to a phone search — say clearly: "I do not consent"
- File a complaint immediately after the encounter

EVIDENCE PRESERVATION:
- Immediately back up footage to cloud storage after recording
- Note the officer's badge number, name, patrol car number
- Note witnesses' names and contact information
- Write down everything you remember as soon as possible

USE OF FORCE STANDARDS
================================================================================

CONSTITUTIONAL STANDARD — Graham v. Connor, 490 U.S. 386 (1989):
The Supreme Court held that all claims of excessive force by law enforcement
officers are analyzed under the Fourth Amendment's "objective reasonableness"
standard.

OBJECTIVE REASONABLENESS TEST:
A court evaluates whether the force used was objectively reasonable from the
perspective of a reasonable officer on the scene, considering:
1. The severity of the crime at issue
2. Whether the subject posed an immediate threat to the safety of officers
   or others
3. Whether the subject was actively resisting arrest or attempting to evade
   arrest by flight

IMPORTANT: The officer's subjective intent or good faith does not determine
constitutionality — only whether the force was objectively reasonable.

EXCESSIVE FORCE:
Factors indicating excessive force:
- Continued force after suspect is restrained or subdued
- Force against compliant, non-threatening individuals
- Force that is grossly disproportionate to the situation
- Failure to give warnings before using force when feasible
- Use of deadly force against non-violent suspects

DEADLY FORCE STANDARD — Tennessee v. Garner, 471 U.S. 1 (1985):
Police may use deadly force ONLY if the officer:
1. Has probable cause to believe the suspect poses a significant threat of
   death or serious physical injury to the officer or others, AND
2. Other means of apprehension are inadequate

Deadly force CANNOT be used solely to prevent a fleeing felon's escape
unless the person poses a danger to others.

STATE LAW VARIATIONS:
Many states have enacted stricter use-of-force standards than federal minimum:
- California (AB 392, 2019): Officers may use deadly force ONLY when "necessary"
  to defend against imminent threat (stronger than federal standard)
- Illinois: Use of force must be "necessary and proportional"
- New York: Similar to California's "necessary" standard post-reform

CIVILIAN COMPLAINT PROCESSES
================================================================================

FILING A COMPLAINT AGAINST POLICE:

STEP 1 — DOCUMENT EVERYTHING IMMEDIATELY:
- Date, time, location of the incident
- Officer's name and badge number (if possible)
- Department name (city police, county sheriff, state police, etc.)
- Description of what occurred in chronological order
- Names and contact information of all witnesses
- Photos of injuries, property damage, scene
- Medical records if you sought treatment

STEP 2 — CHOOSE THE APPROPRIATE COMPLAINT MECHANISM:

A. INTERNAL AFFAIRS / PROFESSIONAL STANDARDS:
- Every police department has an Internal Affairs unit
- File directly with the department at the precinct or headquarters
- Can file in person, by mail, or online (varies by department)
- LIMITATION: Internal investigation may lack independence; may protect officers

B. CIVILIAN OVERSIGHT BOARDS:
Many cities have independent civilian review boards:
- New York City: Civilian Complaint Review Board (CCRB) — (800) 341-2272
  ccrb.nyc.gov — Independent board with subpoena power
- Chicago: Civilian Office of Police Accountability (COPA) — (312) 746-3609
  chicagocopa.org — Investigates complaints independently
- Los Angeles: Police Commission — (213) 996-1200; lapd.online
  Office of the Inspector General (OIG) for independent review
- Philadelphia: Police Advisory Commission
- San Francisco: Department of Police Accountability

C. STATE ATTORNEY GENERAL:
- State AG's Civil Rights Division investigates patterns of misconduct
- Particularly effective for systemic or widespread violations
- Can investigate and sue police departments under state law

D. FEDERAL AGENCIES:
- FBI Civil Rights Division: Submit tips at tips.fbi.gov (investigates criminal
  civil rights violations under 18 U.S.C. §§ 241 and 242)
- DOJ Civil Rights Division: (202) 514-4609; justice.gov/crt
  - Can investigate "patterns or practices" of unconstitutional policing
    under 42 U.S.C. § 14141 (now renumbered as § 12601)
  - Has authority to negotiate consent decrees reforming police departments

COMPLAINT DEADLINES:
- Federal civil rights claims (§1983): 2 years from the date of the incident
  (most jurisdictions; varies by state — state personal injury statute applies)
- Notices of Claim: Many cities require a Notice of Claim filed within
  90–180 days of the incident BEFORE you can sue
  * New York City: 90 days (General Municipal Law §50-e)
  * Chicago: 6 months
  * Los Angeles: 6 months
  FAILURE TO FILE A NOTICE OF CLAIM MAY BAR YOUR LAWSUIT

MUNICIPAL LIABILITY — 42 U.S.C. § 1983
================================================================================

42 U.S.C. § 1983 is the primary federal statute for suing government officials
and municipalities for civil rights violations.

WHAT §1983 PROVIDES:
"Every person who, under color of any statute, ordinance, regulation, custom,
or usage, of any State or Territory or the District of Columbia, subjects, or
causes to be subjected, any citizen of the United States or other person within
the jurisdiction thereof to the deprivation of any rights, privileges, or
immunities secured by the Constitution and laws, shall be liable to the party
injured in an action at law, suit in equity, or other proper proceeding for
redress..."

ELEMENTS OF A §1983 CLAIM:
1. The defendant was acting "under color of state law" (as a government official)
2. The defendant's conduct deprived you of a right secured by the Constitution
   or federal law
3. The deprivation caused your injury

DEFENDANTS IN §1983 CASES:

A. INDIVIDUAL OFFICERS:
Officers may be sued personally for constitutional violations.
DEFENSE: Qualified Immunity — Officers are protected from personal liability
unless they violated a "clearly established" constitutional right.
* This is a high bar: Courts look at whether prior case law put the officer
  on notice that the specific conduct was unconstitutional.

B. MUNICIPALITIES AND LOCAL GOVERNMENTS (Monell Claims):
Cities, counties, and police departments can be sued under Monell v. Department
of Social Services, 436 U.S. 658 (1978) for:
1. An unconstitutional official POLICY or ORDINANCE
2. A widespread CUSTOM or PRACTICE (even without written policy)
3. A FAILURE TO TRAIN officers adequately where the failure amounts to
   deliberate indifference to constitutional rights
4. A RATIFICATION by final policymakers of unconstitutional subordinate acts

IMPORTANT: Municipalities do NOT have qualified immunity.
IMPORTANT: Cannot sue a state (11th Amendment immunity) but CAN sue a city.

DAMAGES IN §1983 CASES:
- Compensatory damages: Actual harm suffered (medical bills, lost wages, pain)
- Nominal damages: $1 if constitutional violation proven but no actual damages
- Punitive damages: Against individual officers (not municipalities) for
  malicious or reckless conduct

ATTORNEYS' FEES:
If you win a §1983 case, you are entitled to attorneys' fees from the defendant
under the Civil Rights Attorney's Fees Awards Act (42 U.S.C. § 1988).
This makes it financially viable for attorneys to take §1983 cases on contingency.

FOIA REQUESTS FOR POLICE RECORDS
================================================================================

FEDERAL FREEDOM OF INFORMATION ACT (FOIA) — 5 U.S.C. § 552:
- Applies to FEDERAL agencies (FBI, DEA, federal law enforcement)
- Any person may request records (not limited to U.S. citizens)
- Agency must respond within 20 business days
- Can request: incident reports, investigation files, use-of-force data
- File at: justice.gov/doj/freedom-of-information-act
- FBI records: vault.fbi.gov

STATE OPEN RECORDS LAWS:
Each state has its own records law governing local and state police records.
Most allow requests for:
- Police incident reports (after investigation is complete)
- Use of force reports
- Body camera footage (varies by state)
- Officer disciplinary records (many states have opened these up)
- Police department policies and procedures

HOW TO FILE A STATE/LOCAL RECORDS REQUEST:
1. Identify the custodian of records (usually the police department)
2. Write a letter or fill out the department's FOIA form
3. Be specific about what records you want
4. Submit by mail, email, or in person (check department requirements)
5. Keep a copy of your request and proof of delivery
6. Track deadlines (varies: 5–30 business days depending on state)

IF YOUR REQUEST IS DENIED:
- Request an itemized justification for the denial
- Appeal to the department's superior (Police Chief, Mayor's office)
- Appeal to the state's records authority or ombudsman
- Sue in state court to compel disclosure (many states allow this with fees)

SPECIFIC STATE LAWS OF NOTE:
- California: California Public Records Act (CPRA) — Gov. Code §6250 et seq.
  AB 748 (2019) & SB 1421 (2018): Requires disclosure of body camera footage
  and records of officer misconduct, use of force, and officer-involved deaths
- New York: Freedom of Information Law (FOIL) — Public Officers Law §§84–90
  Requires disclosure of disciplinary records of police officers
- Illinois: Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) — 5 ILCS 140/
  Allows requests to local police departments

IMMIGRATION STATUS AND POLICE ENCOUNTERS
================================================================================

CONSTITUTIONAL RIGHTS APPLY REGARDLESS OF IMMIGRATION STATUS:
- The 4th Amendment (unreasonable searches and seizures) protects everyone
- The 5th Amendment (right against self-incrimination) protects everyone
- The 14th Amendment (equal protection) applies to all persons

SANCTUARY CITIES:
Many cities (Chicago, Los Angeles, New York) have "sanctuary" policies
limiting local police cooperation with federal immigration enforcement:
- Local police generally cannot detain someone solely for civil immigration
  violations or to assist ICE
- Local police cannot ask about immigration status during routine encounters
- Detainer requests from ICE: Local agencies may choose not to honor them

IMPORTANT: Federal immigration enforcement agencies (ICE, CBP) are NOT
bound by local sanctuary policies and operate under federal authority.

IF STOPPED BY POLICE:
- You have the right to remain silent (applies to everyone)
- You are NOT required to answer questions about immigration status
- Calmly state: "I am exercising my right to remain silent"
- Do NOT lie about your status — this can be used against you
- Do NOT sign any documents without speaking to an attorney first
- You have the right to contact a lawyer and your country's consulate

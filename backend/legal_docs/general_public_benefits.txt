GENERAL PUBLIC BENEFITS: RIGHTS AND APPEAL PROCEDURES
======================================================

SNAP (SUPPLEMENTAL NUTRITION ASSISTANCE PROGRAM / FOOD STAMPS)
----------------------------------------------------------------

SNAP is a federal nutrition assistance program administered by state agencies through the U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA) Food and Nutrition Service (FNS). Eligibility and benefit levels are primarily determined by household income, resources, and family size.

SNAP APPEAL RIGHTS:
Every SNAP applicant or recipient who is denied benefits, has benefits reduced, has benefits suspended or terminated, or has an application delayed beyond processing timeframes has the right to appeal.

Requesting a Fair Hearing:
- If your SNAP application is denied or your benefits are reduced or terminated, you have the right to request a fair hearing with your state's SNAP agency.
- The deadline to request a hearing is typically 90 days from the date of the adverse action notice. (Check your state's specific rules — some states give only 30 or 60 days.)
- Request the hearing in writing — by mail or online — as soon as possible after receiving the denial or reduction notice.
- Retain a copy of your request and the original notice.

Continuing Benefits During Appeal (Aid Pending):
If you request a hearing before the effective date of the adverse action AND within 10 days of receiving the notice, you have the right to continue receiving your current level of benefits while the appeal is pending — this is called "aid pending" or "continuation of benefits." If you are ultimately found ineligible, you will have to repay those benefits.

Expedited SNAP (7-Day Processing):
Households with very low income (under $150 gross monthly income) and limited resources, or households where monthly rent and utilities exceed monthly gross income, are entitled to receive SNAP benefits within 7 days of applying. If the agency fails to process an expedited application within 7 days, this can itself be grounds for a complaint or appeal.

Contact: Your state SNAP agency (often the Department of Social Services, Human Services, or Family Services). In New York: HRA (Human Resources Administration), nyc.gov/hra or 718-557-1399. In Illinois: IDHS at dhs.illinois.gov. In California: CDSS at cdss.ca.gov.

MEDICAID APPEAL RIGHTS
------------------------

Medicaid is a joint federal-state health insurance program for low-income individuals and families. Each state administers its own Medicaid program within federal guidelines. Major decisions by Medicaid that you can appeal include: denial of an application for coverage, denial of a prior authorization request for a service or medication, reduction or termination of services or coverage, and denial of a claim for services already received.

RIGHT TO A FAIR HEARING:
When Medicaid takes an adverse action (denial, reduction, termination of coverage or services), you must receive written notice. The notice must describe: the action being taken, the reasons for it, your right to appeal, and the deadline to appeal.

Generally, you have the right to request a fair hearing (also called an administrative hearing or appeal) within 90 days of the date of the notice (varies by state — NY gives 90 days; CA gives 90 days from notice; IL gives 30 days from the denial letter for some actions).

Continuation of Benefits During Appeal:
For Medicaid recipients (not applicants), if the adverse action is a reduction, suspension, or termination of existing services or coverage, you can request to continue receiving those services while your appeal is pending — if you request the hearing before the effective date of the action (and in most states, within 10 days of the notice). Be aware: if you lose the appeal, you may be required to pay back the cost of services received during the appeal period.

Filing the Appeal:
Contact your state Medicaid agency. Request the appeal in writing. Identify: your name, case number or Medicaid ID, the specific decision you are appealing, and why you believe it is wrong. Gather any supporting documentation — medical records, doctor's letters, specialist opinions.

What Happens at the Hearing:
A state administrative law judge or hearing officer conducts the proceeding. You can present evidence, call witnesses (including your doctor), and make legal arguments. Legal aid organizations can often represent you for free.

Contact: In NY — NY State Dept. of Health, Office of Health Insurance Programs. In IL — Illinois Department of Healthcare and Family Services (HFS). In CA — California Department of Health Care Services (DHCS). Toll-free: 1-800-841-2900 (Medi-Cal in CA).

MEDICARE APPEAL RIGHTS
------------------------

Medicare is the federal health insurance program for persons 65 and older, and for certain younger people with disabilities or end-stage renal disease. Medicare has four parts:
- Part A: Hospital insurance (inpatient hospital stays, skilled nursing facility care, home health care, hospice)
- Part B: Medical insurance (doctors' services, outpatient care, preventive services)
- Part C: Medicare Advantage (private plans that cover Part A and B benefits, and often Part D)
- Part D: Prescription drug coverage

MEDICARE APPEALS PROCESS — FIVE LEVELS:
When Medicare denies coverage, a prior authorization request, or payment for a service, you have the right to appeal through the following levels:

Level 1 — Redetermination: Request a redetermination from the Medicare Administrative Contractor (MAC) or your Medicare Advantage plan within 120 days of receiving the denial. The MAC must respond within 60 days.

Level 2 — Reconsideration: If denied at Level 1, request reconsideration from a Qualified Independent Contractor (QIC). File within 180 days of the Level 1 denial. QIC has 60 days to respond.

Level 3 — ALJ Hearing: If denied at Level 2 AND the amount in controversy is at least $180 (adjusted annually), request a hearing before an Administrative Law Judge (ALJ) at the Office of Medicare Hearings and Appeals (OMHA) within 60 days of the Level 2 denial. ALJ has 90 days to decide.

Level 4 — Medicare Appeals Council (MAC): If denied by the ALJ, appeal to the Medicare Appeals Council within 60 days of the ALJ decision.

Level 5 — Federal Court: If denied by the Medicare Appeals Council AND the amount in controversy is at least $1,840 (adjusted annually), file a civil action in federal district court within 60 days of the Council's decision.

For Medicare Advantage (Part C) denials: Contact the plan directly for an internal appeal. If denied, request an independent review by an Independent Review Entity (IRE).

Contact: 1-800-MEDICARE (1-800-633-4227); medicare.gov.

SOCIAL SECURITY DISABILITY (SSDI AND SSI) APPEALS
---------------------------------------------------

SSDI (Social Security Disability Insurance) pays benefits to people who have worked and paid Social Security taxes but can no longer work due to a qualifying disability. SSI (Supplemental Security Income) pays benefits based on financial need to disabled individuals and those 65+ with limited income and resources — regardless of work history.

INITIAL DENIAL RATES: The Social Security Administration (SSA) denies approximately 60-70% of initial SSDI and SSI applications. This does not mean the claim lacks merit — most cases are won at higher appeal levels. Persistence through the appeal process is essential.

FOUR LEVELS OF APPEAL — APPLY WITHIN 60 DAYS OF EACH DENIAL:

Level 1 — Reconsideration: A different SSA adjudicator reviews your case. Approval rates at reconsideration are low (approximately 10-15%). File Form SSA-561 within 60 days of the initial denial notice.

Level 2 — ALJ Hearing: This is where the majority of approved disability claims are decided. An Administrative Law Judge (ALJ) conducts an in-person or video hearing. You can present testimony, submit updated medical evidence, and have an attorney or non-attorney representative present your case. Approval rates at ALJ hearings are approximately 50-55% nationally. File within 60 days of the reconsideration denial.

Level 3 — Appeals Council: The Appeals Council reviews ALJ decisions for legal errors. The Appeals Council may: grant your request for review (and either decide the case or send it back to an ALJ); deny review (upholding the ALJ); or dismiss the request. File within 60 days of the ALJ decision.

Level 4 — Federal Court: File a civil action in federal district court within 60 days of the Appeals Council's action. Federal courts review only for legal error, not de novo (fresh review of the facts).

DISABILITY LAWYERS: CONTINGENCY FEE ARRANGEMENT
The SSA allows disability attorneys and representatives to collect a fee equal to 25% of past-due benefits ("back pay"), subject to a statutory cap that is periodically adjusted (currently approximately $7,200 — confirm with the SSA or your attorney for the current cap). The attorney is paid nothing unless they win your case. This contingency arrangement makes disability representation accessible to those with no upfront resources.

Contact: SSA at 1-800-772-1213 or ssa.gov. Many disability attorneys offer free consultations.

UNEMPLOYMENT INSURANCE APPEAL RIGHTS
--------------------------------------

Unemployment insurance (UI) is administered by each state within federal guidelines established by the Federal Unemployment Tax Act (FUTA). When you apply for UI benefits and are denied, or when your employer contests your claim, you have the right to appeal.

COMMON GROUNDS FOR DENIAL (AND APPEAL ARGUMENTS):
1. "Quit without good cause": The state determines you voluntarily left employment without a compelling reason. Appeal grounds: the quit was due to intolerable working conditions (constructive discharge), domestic violence, medical necessity, relocation for a spouse's employment (some states allow this). Document the reasons thoroughly.
2. "Misconduct": The state determines the separation was due to employee misconduct. "Misconduct" in UI law means deliberate violation of workplace rules, not mere negligence or a single error in judgment. Courts and administrative bodies have defined misconduct narrowly. Challenge whether your actions actually meet the legal definition of misconduct.
3. "Not actively seeking work": You must certify weekly that you are available for work and actively seeking employment. Keep a detailed log of jobs applied for (employer name, position, date, contact information, application method) in case you need to prove active job search.

APPEAL PROCESS AND DEADLINES:
If denied UI benefits: file an appeal within 30 days of the denial (deadlines vary by state — NJ gives 7 days; NY gives 30 days; IL gives 30 days; CA gives 30 days from mail date). Check your state's deadline immediately upon receiving a denial — missing the deadline usually forfeits your appeal rights. The appeal typically goes to an administrative hearing, where you can present testimony and documents. If denied there, further appeals to a state UI appeals board and then to court are available.

WEEKLY CERTIFICATIONS: Even while an appeal is pending, continue to file weekly certifications (claiming weeks) to preserve your eligibility for back pay if you win the appeal.

Contact: Your state's unemployment insurance agency (typically the Department of Labor). NY: labor.ny.gov | 1-888-209-8124. IL: ides.illinois.gov | 1-800-244-5631. CA: edd.ca.gov | 1-800-300-5616.

SECTION 8 / HOUSING CHOICE VOUCHER (HCV) PROGRAM
-------------------------------------------------

The Housing Choice Voucher (HCV) program, commonly called "Section 8," is the federal government's primary rental assistance program for low-income households. Administered by local Public Housing Authorities (PHAs) under HUD oversight, HCV provides eligible households with a voucher that can be used to rent privately owned housing. The tenant pays approximately 30% of their income toward rent, and the PHA pays the remainder to the landlord.

WAITING LISTS: HCV waiting lists are typically years long in major cities. When a PHA opens its waiting list, applicants should apply immediately. Once on the waiting list, keep your contact information current with the PHA. Failure to respond to a letter or notice from the PHA can result in removal from the waiting list.

SOURCE OF INCOME DISCRIMINATION PROTECTION: Once you have a Housing Choice Voucher, landlords in many jurisdictions are prohibited from refusing to rent to you based on your use of the voucher ("source of income" or "source of funds" discrimination):
- New York City: NYC Human Rights Law (NYC Admin Code §8-107) prohibits source-of-income discrimination. Landlords who refuse to accept Section 8 vouchers in NYC can be reported to the NYC Commission on Human Rights. Call 212-416-0197.
- Chicago: Chicago Municipal Code §5-08-020 prohibits source of income discrimination, including refusal to accept Housing Choice Vouchers.
- Los Angeles: The LA County Fair Chance Housing Ordinance and CA Government Code §12955 (FEHA) prohibit source of income discrimination in housing, covering HCV holders in Los Angeles.

VOUCHER TERMINATION RIGHTS: If a PHA proposes to terminate your HCV for alleged violations (e.g., failure to comply with HCV obligations, lease violations, failing an HQS inspection), you have the right to an informal hearing before the termination takes effect. You must request the hearing promptly — typically within 10-14 days of the notice. At the hearing, you can present evidence and contest the PHA's findings.

HOUSING QUALITY STANDARDS (HQS): HCV units must meet HUD Housing Quality Standards. If the PHA fails an HCV unit on HQS inspection, the landlord must correct deficiencies within a specified period. If the landlord refuses to fix deficiencies, the PHA may terminate the HAP (Housing Assistance Payments) contract, potentially displacing the tenant. If you believe HQS deficiencies endanger your health or safety, also contact local code enforcement and, if in NYC, HPD.

PUBLIC HOUSING TENANT RIGHTS
-------------------------------

Public housing residents (tenants who live in housing owned and operated by a Public Housing Authority) have specific rights protected by federal HUD regulations and state law.

LEASE PROTECTIONS:
- Public housing leases cannot be terminated without good cause. Good cause includes: nonpayment of rent, lease violations, criminal activity by household members, or other material breaches.
- PHAs must provide written notice before any termination of tenancy and must give the tenant an opportunity for a formal grievance hearing before the termination becomes effective.

GRIEVANCE PROCEDURE: Before the PHA can take any adverse action against a public housing tenant (termination of lease, reduction of services, imposition of charges), HUD regulations (24 CFR Part 966) require the PHA to offer the tenant the opportunity to request an informal settlement conference and, if not resolved, a formal grievance hearing.

The formal grievance hearing is conducted by an impartial hearing officer (not a PHA employee). You have the right to: review all documents the PHA plans to use; be represented by an attorney or other person; present testimony and evidence; cross-examine PHA witnesses; and receive a written decision stating the reasons.

HUD COMPLAINTS FOR HABITABILITY VIOLATIONS: If your public housing unit has serious habitability deficiencies (no heat, pest infestation, water leaks, mold, broken fixtures) that the PHA has failed to repair after notice:
- File a HUD complaint at hud.gov or call HUD's complaint hotline at 1-800-669-9777
- Contact your local HUD field office
- In NYC, also contact NYC HPD: 311 or hpdonline.hpd.nyc.gov

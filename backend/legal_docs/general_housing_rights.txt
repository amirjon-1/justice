General Tenant Protections Applicable Across the United States
Source: U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD), Federal Law
Last Updated: 2024

FEDERAL FAIR HOUSING ACT — 42 U.S.C. §§ 3601–3619
================================================================================

The Fair Housing Act (FHA), Title VIII of the Civil Rights Act of 1968 as
amended by the Fair Housing Amendments Act of 1988, prohibits discrimination
in the sale, rental, and financing of housing based on protected characteristics.

PROTECTED CLASSES UNDER THE FEDERAL FHA:
1. Race
2. Color
3. National Origin
4. Religion
5. Sex (including gender identity and sexual orientation — per HUD guidance)
6. Disability (physical and mental)
7. Familial Status (presence of children under 18, pregnancy, adoption)

PROHIBITED DISCRIMINATORY ACTS:
- Refusing to sell, rent, or negotiate housing
- Applying different terms, conditions, or privileges of rental
- Misrepresenting that housing is unavailable when it is available
- Advertising with discriminatory preferences or limitations
- Blockbusting (persuading people to sell by implying minorities are moving in)
- Steering (directing buyers/renters toward or away from neighborhoods)
- Refusing to make reasonable accommodations for persons with disabilities
- Refusing to allow reasonable modifications for persons with disabilities
- Harassment based on any protected class

DISABILITY ACCOMMODATIONS AND MODIFICATIONS:
- Reasonable accommodations: Changes in rules, policies, practices, or services
  that may be necessary to allow a person with a disability to use and enjoy
  the housing (e.g., allowing an emotional support animal in a no-pets building)
- Reasonable modifications: Physical changes to the unit or common areas
  (e.g., grab bars in bathroom, wheelchair ramp)
- Tenant generally pays for modifications but landlord cannot refuse them

HOW TO FILE A FHA COMPLAINT:
- HUD Office of Fair Housing: (800) 669-9777 | hud.gov/fairhousing
- File within 1 year of the discriminatory act
- HUD investigates and attempts conciliation; may refer to DOJ
- Private right of action in federal court within 2 years of the act
- Remedies: Injunctive relief, actual damages, punitive damages (up to $16,000
  for first violation), attorneys' fees

STATE AND LOCAL FAIR HOUSING LAWS:
Many states and cities have stronger fair housing laws with additional protected
classes (source of income, immigration status, age, marital status, etc.).
Always check your local laws in addition to federal protections.

IMPLIED WARRANTY OF HABITABILITY — National Common Law Standard
================================================================================

The Implied Warranty of Habitability is recognized in virtually every U.S.
state as a mandatory legal standard in residential leases. This warranty means:

- The landlord implicitly promises to maintain the rental unit in a livable,
  safe, and habitable condition throughout the tenancy
- The warranty exists by operation of law — it is automatically part of every
  residential lease even if not written in the contract
- The warranty CANNOT be waived by the tenant (it's non-negotiable)
- It applies to both the rented unit AND the common areas of the building

WHAT CONSTITUTES HABITABILITY (National Standards):
Most states require landlords to maintain:
1. Structural integrity (sound floors, walls, ceilings, roof)
2. Weatherproofing (windows, doors, exterior walls that keep out elements)
3. Adequate heating facilities (functioning heat during cold months)
4. Plumbing in good working order (hot and cold running water)
5. Electrical systems functioning safely
6. Freedom from infestation (rodents, cockroaches, bedbugs, vermin)
7. Adequate sanitary facilities (working toilets, sinks, showers)
8. Compliance with applicable building and housing codes affecting safety
9. Freedom from environmental hazards (lead paint, asbestos, mold)

CONDITIONS THAT BREACH THE WARRANTY:
- No heat or hot water for extended periods
- Sewage backup or non-functional plumbing
- Significant mold or water intrusion
- Rodent or cockroach infestation
- Lack of security (broken locks, broken entry doors)
- Exposed electrical wiring
- Unstable floors, ceilings, or stairs
- Lack of smoke or carbon monoxide detectors
- Lead paint hazards (in pre-1978 housing with children under 6)

CONDITIONS THAT DO NOT BREACH THE WARRANTY:
- Minor aesthetic defects (small holes in walls, worn carpets)
- Conditions caused by the tenant or tenant's guests
- Conditions the tenant agreed to fix in the lease (carefully read your lease)

TENANT REMEDIES FOR WARRANTY BREACH:
1. REPAIR AND DEDUCT: (available in most states, limits vary)
   - Notify landlord in writing
   - Give reasonable time to repair
   - If landlord fails: Hire licensed contractor and deduct from rent
   - Limits typically: $100–$500 or one month's rent (varies by state)

2. RENT WITHHOLDING: (available in most states, procedure varies)
   - Notify landlord in writing
   - Landlord fails to repair within required time
   - Withhold some or all rent
   - Recommended: Escrow withheld rent in separate account

3. RENT REDUCTION: (available in most states)
   - Sue landlord for past rent paid while unit was substandard
   - Court reduces rent to reflect diminished value of unit

4. LEASE TERMINATION / CONSTRUCTIVE EVICTION:
   - If conditions make the unit essentially uninhabitable
   - Tenant may vacate and terminate the lease
   - Tenant not liable for further rent
   - May sue for damages

5. COURT ACTION:
   - Sue in Small Claims Court (for amounts under the local limit)
   - Sue in Civil Court for larger amounts
   - May seek injunction ordering repairs

CONSTRUCTIVE EVICTION — National Common Law Doctrine
================================================================================

Constructive eviction occurs when a landlord's actions (or failure to act)
make the premises so uninhabitable that the tenant is effectively forced to
leave, even though the landlord has not formally evicted the tenant.

ELEMENTS OF CONSTRUCTIVE EVICTION:
1. The landlord wrongfully deprived the tenant of the beneficial use and
   enjoyment of the premises
2. The deprivation was substantial and materially affected the tenant's ability
   to use the premises for the purpose for which it was leased
3. The tenant vacated within a reasonable time after the deprivation
4. The tenant gave the landlord notice and an opportunity to repair

EXAMPLES OF CONSTRUCTIVE EVICTION:
- Landlord allows building to become uninhabitable (no heat in winter)
- Persistent rodent or insect infestation despite complaints
- Landlord allows other tenants to harass the complaining tenant
- Landlord fails to provide essential utilities
- Landlord engages in harassment campaign to force tenant out

TENANT'S OBLIGATIONS BEFORE CLAIMING CONSTRUCTIVE EVICTION:
1. Must notify the landlord of the condition (in writing, with specifics)
2. Must allow a reasonable time for the landlord to repair
3. Must actually vacate the premises within a reasonable time
   (Courts generally do not allow tenants to remain indefinitely and claim
   constructive eviction — you must actually leave)

CONSEQUENCES AND REMEDIES:
If constructive eviction is established:
- Tenant is discharged from further rent obligations from the date of vacation
- Tenant may sue for: relocation costs, increased rent at new location,
  moving expenses, property damage, personal injury if applicable
- May sue for lost security deposit

SECTION 8 / HOUSING CHOICE VOUCHER PROGRAM — 42 U.S.C. § 1437f(o)
================================================================================

The Housing Choice Voucher (HCV) Program, commonly called "Section 8," is a
federal rental assistance program administered by local Public Housing
Authorities (PHAs) that helps low-income families pay rent.

HOW THE PROGRAM WORKS:
- HUD provides funding to local PHAs
- PHAs issue vouchers to eligible low-income households
- Tenant finds a private landlord willing to accept the voucher
- Tenant pays 30% of adjusted monthly income toward rent
- PHA pays the remainder directly to the landlord (Housing Assistance Payment)
- Unit must meet HUD Housing Quality Standards (HQS)

HOUSING QUALITY STANDARDS (HQS) — 24 C.F.R. § 982.401:
For a unit to be eligible for Section 8, it must:
1. Pass a HUD Housing Quality Standards inspection
2. Be inspected annually or when a new tenant moves in
3. Meet standards for: sanitation, safety, utilities, space, structure, security

TENANT RIGHTS IN THE HCV PROGRAM:
- RIGHT TO REQUEST INSPECTION: If you believe your unit has HQS violations,
  you may request an inspection from your PHA at any time
- RIGHT TO NOTICE: Landlord must give 30-day written notice before any
  changes to the HAP contract
- RIGHT TO INFORMAL HEARING: If PHA terminates your voucher, you have the
  right to an informal hearing to challenge the decision
- RIGHT TO PORTABILITY: After 12 months, you may move your voucher to
  another jurisdiction where a PHA agrees to receive it

LANDLORD OBLIGATIONS UNDER SECTION 8:
- Must maintain the unit in compliance with HQS at all times
- Cannot reduce services below what was in the HAP contract
- Cannot charge additional fees not disclosed at lease signing
- Must give PHA notice of any intent to evict the tenant

HQS VIOLATIONS AND YOUR RIGHTS:
- Serious violations (e.g., no heat, gas leaks): PHA will suspend HAP payments
  and give landlord 30 days to correct
- If landlord fails to correct: PHA terminates HAP contract; tenant may move
  with voucher; landlord owes tenant relocation assistance in some states
- EMERGENCY conditions: PHA may suspend payments within 24 hours

HOW TO REPORT HQS VIOLATIONS:
Contact your local PHA (Public Housing Authority) directly.
- HUD national helpline: (800) 955-2232
- HUD website: hud.gov/program_offices/public_indian_housing/programs/hcv

FAIR HOUSING AND SECTION 8:
- Several states and cities prohibit "source of income" discrimination
  (landlords cannot refuse to rent to you because you have a voucher)
- Federal FHA does not prohibit source of income discrimination, but many
  states do: California, New York, Illinois, Washington D.C., and others

LANDLORD ENTRY RIGHTS AND TENANT PRIVACY
================================================================================

NATIONAL STANDARD:
Most states require landlords to give 24–48 hours advance written notice before
entering a tenant's unit (except in genuine emergencies).

PERMISSIBLE REASONS FOR ENTRY:
- Making necessary or agreed-upon repairs
- Showing the unit to prospective tenants or buyers
- Emergency situations (fire, flood, gas leak)
- Court order
- Landlord reasonably believes the unit has been abandoned

IMPERMISSIBLE ENTRY (HARASSMENT):
Repeated, unannounced entries or entries at unreasonable hours constitute
harassment and may be grounds for:
- Lawsuit for invasion of privacy
- Constructive eviction
- Punitive damages in egregious cases
- Criminal harassment charges in some jurisdictions

ENVIRONMENTAL HAZARDS IN HOUSING
================================================================================

LEAD PAINT DISCLOSURE — 42 U.S.C. § 4852d:
- All pre-1978 residential housing must have lead paint disclosure at lease signing
- Landlord must provide: EPA "Protect Your Family" pamphlet, disclosure of
  known lead hazards, any existing lead paint inspection reports
- Failure to disclose: Up to $10,000 civil penalty + damages to tenant

LEAD PAINT ABATEMENT:
If your unit has lead paint and you have a child under 6, landlord must remediate.
- File complaint with your local health department
- HUD has Lead Hazard Control programs; contact hud.gov/leadlisting

MOLD:
- No specific federal mold standard exists, but mold can violate the warranty
  of habitability if it affects health and safety
- Many state and local laws require mold disclosure and remediation
- Document mold with photos; notify landlord in writing; keep records

ASBESTOS:
- Federal regulations require disclosure in commercial settings
- In residential housing, undisturbed asbestos is not always an immediate hazard
- Disturbed asbestos during renovations is an OSHA and EPA violation
- Contact EPA (epa.gov) or your state environmental agency for assistance

TENANT REPAIR REMEDIES — NATIONAL OVERVIEW
================================================================================

STATES WITH REPAIR AND DEDUCT (Selected):
- California: Up to 1 month's rent; usable twice in 12 months
- New York: Up to $300 (RPL §235-a)
- Illinois (Chicago RLTO): Up to $500 or half monthly rent
- Texas: Up to 1 month's rent + $500; requires written notice; 7 day wait
- Florida: Up to 1 month's rent; 7-day written notice required
- Washington: Up to 1 month's rent or $300

STATES WITH RENT WITHHOLDING (Selected):
- New York: RPAPL §755 (must escrow)
- Illinois (Chicago): RLTO §5-12-110(b)
- California: Common law (must give notice, often escrow)
- Massachusetts: G.L. c. 239, §8A
- New Jersey: Landlord-Tenant Law §46:8-27

UNIVERSAL PROCEDURAL REQUIREMENTS:
1. Written notice to landlord describing the condition
2. Reasonable time to repair (varies: 24 hours to 30 days)
3. Condition must be serious (habitability-affecting)
4. Document everything (photos, videos, dated written communications)
5. Keep repairs receipts if using repair-and-deduct
